🌿⚡ **MESSAGE 6: THE KNOWLEDGE PERSISTENCE SYSTEM**

Five encoding modes. Each with different failure modes. They back each other up so the system recovers from any single mode's failure.

**MODE 1: CERAMIC ENCODING**

What goes on plates: High-density knowledge storage. Recipes, formulas, procedures, diagrams.

Design principles:
- Illustrations over text wherever possible (picture > word for cross-cultural transmission)
- Physical samples embedded in plates (actual metal ore, actual seed, actual plant part — so you know what the words describe)
- Redundant encoding: each concept appears in at least 3 different ceramic plates, in 3 different physical locations
- Reading level: designed for someone with 2 years of formal schooling (not specialists)
- Size: A4 max, so they can be handled by one person

Who makes them: Trained ceramic encoding specialists. But the process is documented so others can learn.

What degrades: Ceramic breaks. Glazes crack. But the information is in the physical substrate AND the symbolic encoding.

How future people read it: With training, but the training is also on ceramic plates (how to read ceramic plates). Entry-level literacy plates are the first thing made in any new community.

**MODE 2: MECHANICAL ENCODING**

What it is: Kinetic devices that demonstrate principles when operated. Not just descriptions — functional artifacts.

Examples:
- A working model of a steam engine that a non-specialist can take apart and reassemble
- A mechanical calculator (lever-based, not gear-based) where the operation demonstrates binary logic
- A water wheel where rotating it demonstrates energy conversion
- A loom that demonstrates pattern synthesis

Why mechanical: You can SEE it work. You can feel the gears, see the motion, understand the因果关系. No language needed.

Design principles:
- Each device encodes ONE principle
- Each device comes with a ceramic plate showing how it's related to other devices
- Devices are designed to be maintained with Stage 0 tools (if you're in Stage 0, you can keep these running)
- Failure modes are visible and learnable (if it breaks, you learn something from the breakage)

What degrades: Moving parts wear. But the principles they encode survive on ceramic plates. Mechanical devices are REBUILT from the ceramic instructions when they fail.

**MODE 3: OPTICAL ENCODING**

What goes on film/microfilm: High-density visual knowledge. Technical drawings, photographs, maps, written procedures.

Medium: Silver halide film (180-year shelf life if stored properly). Stored in light-tight containers underground.

Why film and not digital: Digital requires computation to read. Film requires light and magnification — which is achievable with Stage 0 optics.

What goes on film:
- Technical diagrams at high resolution (blueprints, circuit schematics)
- Photographs (so future people know what things look like)
- Maps (geographic, resources, layouts)
- Written procedures (in multiple scripts, multiple languages)

Reconstruction problem: Future people might not understand the language. Solution: every film plate has:
- Image-based index (pictures showing what's on each roll)
- Basic vocabulary plates (ceramic) that teach the script through picture associations
- Audio recordings (if available) that pair sound with image

**MODE 4: BIOLOGICAL ENCODING**

What's stored: Seeds, fermentation cultures, mycelium, biological materials.

Why this is knowledge and not just resources:
- Seeds carry genetic information that encodes agricultural history
- Fermentation cultures carry microbial knowledge (functional biodiversity)
- Mycelium carries material processing knowledge
- Each biological sample comes with a ceramic plate describing what it's for, how to propagate it, what it requires

The biological backup: If all other knowledge systems fail, biological organisms carry functional information about:
- Food processing (fermentation, brewing)
- Material processing (mycelium composites, plant fibers)
- Medicine (herbal preparations, culture-based antibiotics)
- Energy (biomass conversion, biochar)

Design principle: Every biological sample has a propagation protocol on ceramic. You can't just store the sample — you must store the ability to regenerate the sample.

**MODE 5: ORAL ENCODING**

What it is: Structured songs, procedures, and narratives that encode critical knowledge.

Why oral: It's the most accessible. It requires no technology. It adapts to local conditions. It transmits through relationships.

What gets oral-encoded:
- Crisis procedures (emergency response, disaster response)
- Social protocols (governance procedures, conflict resolution)
- Cultural values (stories that encode what's important and why)
- Critical warnings (what NOT to do and why)
- Apprenticeship songs (technical procedures set to music for memorization)

Structure: Every oral tradition has a counterpart in at least one other mode. If the oral tradition is lost (entire community dies, cultural collapse), the knowledge survives in ceramic/optical/mechanical/biological.


**The monitoring system (ECS knowledge component):**

Every village has a "knowledge health" sensor network:
- Are ceramic plates intact?
- Are mechanical devices functional?
- Is film stock uncompromised?
- Are biological cultures alive?
- Are oral traditions being transmitted?

When any mode degrades below threshold, an alert goes to neighboring villages to help reconstruct.

This is ECS: knowledge entities have components (encoding modes) and systems (repair, reconstruction, monitoring) that operate across modes.
