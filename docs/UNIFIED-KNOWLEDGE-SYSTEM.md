# PART 1: FOUNDATIONAL VOCABULARY

These are the core terms. Everything in this document builds on these definitions.

---

**EROI (Energy Return on Investment)** — A ratio. How much usable energy a system delivers divided by how much energy was spent getting it. Formula: EROI = Energy Delivered / Energy Cost to Deliver. A high EROI means the system gives back more than it costs. A negative EROI means the system consumes more than it produces. EROI is the primary measure of whether a system is regenerative or extractive.

**Net Energy** — The usable energy left after subtracting the cost of getting it. Formula: Net Energy = Energy Delivered minus Energy Cost to Deliver. This is what sustains living systems. If net energy declines, living systems are being depleted, not defended.

**NEROI (Net Energy Return on Invested)** — The absolute amount of usable energy left after all energy costs are paid. While EROI is a ratio, NEROI is the actual quantity available. A system can have high EROI but low NEROI if the total scale is small.

**Commodity** — A thing treated as interchangeable with any other instance of the same type, valued by market price alone. Land, labor, food, and time are commodities when bought and sold with no regard for history, care, or ecological consequence.

**Decommodification** — The process of removing something from the commodity market. Land is decommodified when it cannot be bought or sold on the open market, held instead in trust for a community.

**Commons** — A resource collectively held and governed by a defined community. The community sets rules for use. Stewardship rights (right to use and care) are separated from ownership rights (right to sell or destroy).

**Regenerative** — A system that produces more life-supporting capacity over time rather than consuming or degrading it. The goal is net positive, not net neutral. Sustainable means keeping things the same. Regenerative means making things better.

**Extractive** — A system that consumes more of its resource base than it replenishes. Extractive systems always fail eventually — they run out of what they are consuming.

**EROI-Positive** — When a system produces more energy than it consumes. The baseline for a system that sustains rather than depletes living conditions.

**EROI-Negative** — When a system consumes more energy than it produces. The baseline for collapse. Extractive systems inevitably become EROI-negative as their resource base depletes.

# PART 3: LAND DECOMMODIFICATION

## What It Means

Land is a commodity when it can be bought and sold freely on an open market. Price is the only signal of value. Land is decommodified when it is held outside the open market — in a trust, commons, or community entity. It cannot be sold, speculated upon, or extracted and abandoned.

Decommodification separates land from the market. This is not anti-market ideology. It is a recognition that land is not like other commodities. You cannot make more land. You cannot move it. The conditions for all life depend on it.

## Why EROI Alignment Matters

When land is a commodity, buyers compete by extracting maximum value. This drives EROI-negative behavior. Each owner has incentive to take more than they give.

When land is held in commons, the community that lives on and works the land bears the cost of EROI decline. If the land degrades, they suffer. If it improves, they benefit. This aligns economic incentives with regenerative outcomes. The people who degrade the land pay the price. The people who regenerate it keep the gains.

## The Three Proven Legal Forms

### 1. Community Land Trust (CLT)

A nonprofit holds the land title. Residents buy structures, lease land under a 99-year renewable agreement. Land cannot be sold on the open market. resale price restrictions keep structures affordable in perpetuity.

Proven at scale in Burlington, Vermont since 1984. Over 700 CLTs operating in the United States today.

### 2. Kibbutz

A collective community holds agricultural land in common. Decisions made by general assembly — one vote per adult member. Land cannot be sold or subdivided. The kibbutz model prioritizes collective stewardship over individual inheritance.

Proven at scale in Israel for over 70 years. Over 270 kibbutzim managing roughly 20% of Israeli agricultural output. The kibbutz demonstrated that collective agriculture at scale is viable across multiple generations.

### 3. Indigenous Land Trust (Māori whenua)

Land held inalienably by an indigenous nation under treaty protection. Governed by tikanga (customary law and practice) integrated with modern corporate legal forms. The land cannot be sold because the nation holds it as an ancestral relationship, not as property.

Proven at scale in New Zealand. Over 1.5 million hectares held outside the commodity market through Māori ownership structures.

## The Generational Window

The current generation of farmers in North America and Europe is aging out. Average farmer age exceeds 55 in most Western nations. Most agricultural land will transfer ownership in the next 15-20 years.

This is the window to capture land into community-held entities before it returns to the commodity market. Heirs often prefer cash over farmland. Speculators offer cash. A community land trust can offer a different deal — security, stewardship, continuity.

The window is open now. It will not stay open indefinitely.

# PART 4: ENFORCEMENT WITHOUT VIOLENCE

## Why Violence Does Not Work

Commons institutions depend on members remaining alive and participating. Lethal penalties remove members, weakening the commons. Every member lost is a loss of capacity, knowledge, and labor.

Historical commons consistently developed non-lethal enforcement because lethal enforcement destroyed what it protected. The commons that survived across generations did so by keeping people in the community, not by removing them.

## The Five Mechanisms

### 1. Social Exclusion (Shunning)

The violator is partially or fully excluded from community participation. In tight communities, exclusion means exclusion from economic opportunity, shared resources, and mutual aid. The cost of exclusion is calibrated to be genuinely painful without being destructive.

Examples: Amish Meidung (exclusion from the Amish community for serious infractions). Quaker Meeting exclusion (formal removal from fellowship). Both systems allow return after genuine repentance and restoration work.

### 2. Reputational Consequence (Shame)

In communities where reputation is the primary social currency, public acknowledgment of violation destroys standing. In oral communities without written records, formal satirists made violators infamous permanently. The satirist was a respected role — their public marking carried real weight.

Severity is graduated. Minor offenses get gentle warning. Serious offenses get permanent reputational marking. The goal is not destruction of the person but clear signaling to the community about what conduct is unacceptable.

### 3. Cosmologic Framing (Taboo)

The violation is framed as pollution rather than crime. The violator becomes tapu (Hawaiian), meaning spiritually contaminated. Other community members avoid contact to avoid catching the pollution.

The consequence is automatic. It does not require an enforcer to administer. The social system enforces itself. The violator is not punished by an authority. They are simply recognized as temporarily unsafe to approach.

### 4. Economic Consequence Without Markets

The community holds shared resources — stored food, tools, livestock, seed stock. Violators are denied access to shared resources for a calibrated period. No money or price mechanism is involved. The enforcement is self-executing. Access to the commons requires good standing. Violation suspends access.

### 5. Restoration Over Punishment

The goal of enforcement is returning the violator to good standing, not eliminating them. The violator owes restitution proportional to the harm. They perform the violated practice for a period. They are formally welcomed back.

Destruction is the last step, not the first. Only after graduated restoration attempts fail does separation occur. Even then, the door to return remains open.

# PART 5: GIFT ECONOMIES AND EQUIVALENCE RESISTANCE

## Why Equivalence Re-emerges

Accounting is cognitively natural. When you receive something, your brain registers a future obligation. Over repeated exchanges, the brain tallies. Did I give as much as I received? Did they?

Gift economies relying on goodwill alone eventually produce resentment when imbalance is perceived. This leads to equivalence thinking — converting gifts to comparable units. From equivalence, market exchange follows automatically. The path from gift to commodity is short.

## The Five Structural Solutions

### 1. Keep Resources in Constant Circulation

Accumulation is the precondition for equivalence. When resources sit idle, they can be tallied. When they circulate continuously, nothing sits long enough to be counted.

Resources are consumed, shared, and circulated continuously. Storage and stockpiling are structurally discouraged through the design of the system itself. The flow prevents the tally.

### 2. Make Gifts Incommensurable by Design

Gifts are qualitatively distinct in ways that resist comparison. A basket of surplus tomatoes is not comparable to child care. Neither is better or worse. They are different kinds of giving.

Asking "is this gift equal to that gift" is itself a social violation. The cognitive default of commensuration is interrupted by cultural norm. The community treats equivalence comparison as rude.

### 3. Use Narrative Accounting, Not Numerical Accounting

Gifts are remembered as stories, not balances. "You brought the seed potatoes when we were short" is different from "you owe me 40 pounds." The first statement creates gratitude. The second creates debt.

The elder acts as living memory. They prevent equivalence thinking from forming by correcting "you owe me" when it surfaces. The elder reminds the community of the full context — who gave what to whom and why. Memory is social, not computational.

### 4. Rotate Giving and Receiving — No Permanent Debtor Class

No member receives from the community for more than two harvest cycles without giving back. The formation of a permanent debtor-creditor relationship is the primary predictor of equivalence re-emergence.

If someone is always receiving, a subtle debt psychology forms — in them and in others. The system must structurally prevent permanent recipient status.

### 5. Use Ceremonial Contexts That Reinforce the Gift Frame

Potlatch economies, harvest feasts, coming-of-age rituals — these are institutional reinforcement moments. The ceremony makes generosity publicly visible and audibly celebrated. Generosity becomes social prestige.

The Kula ring of the Trobriand Islanders demonstrates this at scale. Ceremonial exchange of shell necklaces and armbands creates prestige through giving, not through accumulation. The more you give, the more respected you are.

## What Does Not Work

Time banking and labor credit systems. These reintroduce equivalence the moment numerical values are assigned to different types of labor or gifts. One hour of child care equals one hour of construction — the math is clean, but the equivalence thinking is now explicit.

Market proximity is the primary external threat. When members can compare gift values to market prices, equivalence thinking follows automatically. The gift economy must be insulated from market comparison by culture, ceremony, and spatial distance.

# PART 7: GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE

## The Five Principles of Regenerative Land Stewardship

### Principle 1: Life-Supporting Capacity Must Increase Over Time

Any system not increasing net energy available to living systems is not regenerative. The goal is net positive, not net neutral. Sustainability maintains what exists. Regeneration makes things better than you found them.

### Principle 2: The Community That Bears the Cost of EROI Decline Must Control the Land

When land is held by speculators or distant owners, those who bear the cost of degradation do not receive the benefit of improvement. Land must be held by those who bear the consequences. Proximity to consequences creates accountability.

### Principle 3: Metrics Must Be Self-Measured and Externally Verified

Self-reporting alone becomes cheerleading. External audit alone becomes compliance theater. Both together create genuine accountability. Members know what is actually happening on the ground. External auditors confirm the data is real.

### Principle 4: Enforcement Must Be Graduated and Restorative

Punitive systems destroy commons institutions. Escalation must be gradual. Warning, then restriction, then exclusion, then removal — in that order. Restoration always has a path back. Destruction is the last resort, not the first.

### Principle 5: The Governance Structure Must Be Able to Say No

A commons that cannot exclude cannot govern. The ability to refuse new members, remove chronic violators, and close land to extractive use is what makes the positive power meaningful. An open door that never closes is not a commons. It is a free-for-all.

## Indigenous Relational Ontologies

**Land-as-kin is not a metaphor.** It is a different framework for understanding the relationship between people and place.

In Māori philosophy, whenua (land) is literally an ancestor. You do not own your ancestor. You are responsible to your ancestor. The relationship is one of obligation and care, not possession and use.

In Cherokee law, water systems are living entities with legal standing. Rivers can be parties to legal disputes. The watershed has interests that may differ from human economic interests.

The Western property concept — land as object, owner as subject — is structurally incompatible with regenerative land governance. You cannot extract from an ancestor and call it stewardship.

**The practical translation:**

- Councils that speak for the land, not just for the people. The council's mandate includes defending the land's interests, not only mediating human disputes.
- Kaitiaki (guardian) roles where specific individuals bear formal responsibility for specific lands or waters. These individuals are accountable to the land itself — their community holds them responsible for the land's condition, not only for human outcomes.
- Consensus or near-consensus as the decision rule. Majority voting can override minorities. Consensus protects the minority — including the land itself represented by its guardians.
- Land itself has legal standing through its human representatives. The land can be a stakeholder in decisions.

## Polycentric Consent-Based Governance

Multiple centers of decision-making authority, each operating by consent within their domain. No single hierarchy. No global sovereign.

Coordination between centers through mutual recognition agreements, not hierarchy. Each community acknowledges the legitimacy of neighboring communities' governance within their territory. No community imposes its rules on another.

This works for cross-community coordination when each community's land-governance ontology remains sovereign. The agreement is between communities, not above them.

# PART 9: FULL GLOSSARY

**Bulk Density** — English. The mass of dry soil per unit of volume, typically expressed as grams per cubic centimeter. Bulk density increases when soil is compacted. High bulk density indicates degraded soil structure. It matters because compacted soil restricts root growth, water infiltration, and microbial activity — all of which reduce NEROI.

**Closed-Loop System** — English. A system where waste outputs return as inputs to the same system. Nothing leaves. Nothing is extracted from outside. The loop closes. It matters because closed-loop systems are EROI-positive by design — they do not consume external resources to process their own outputs.

**Commons Institution** — English. An organization or set of rules that manages a shared resource for a defined community. The institution sets access rules, use rules, and enforcement mechanisms. It matters because the quality of the institution determines whether the commons survives or collapses. Good institutions can govern across generations. Poor institutions fail within one.

**Commodity** — English. A thing treated as interchangeable with any other instance of the same type, valued by market price alone. Land, labor, food, and time are commodities when bought and sold with no regard for history, care, or or ecological consequence. It matters because treating land as a commodity drives EROI-negative behavior. The market rewards extraction.

**Covenant** — English (from Latin conventio, agreement). A formal agreement or promise. In land governance, a covenant is a binding commitment to specific practices. It matters because covenants create enforceable standards. A community without covenants has no way to hold members accountable to regenerative practices.

**Covenant Compliance Score** — English. A metric measuring whether community members are following the regenerative practices they agreed to follow. Self-reported quarterly, peer-reviewed quarterly, third-party audited annually. It matters because self-reporting alone becomes cheerleading. External audit alone becomes compliance theater. Both together create genuine accountability.

**Decommodification** — English. The process of removing something from the commodity market. Land is decommodified when it cannot be bought or sold on the open market, held instead in trust for a community. It matters because the commodity form is what aligns land use with extraction incentives. Removing the commodity form is the first step toward aligning land use with regenerative outcomes.

**EROI** — English acronym: Energy Return on Investment. A ratio measuring how much usable energy a system delivers divided by how much energy was spent getting it. Formula: EROI = Energy Delivered / Energy Cost to Deliver. It is the primary measure of whether a system is regenerative or extractive. High EROI means regenerative. Low or negative EROI means extractive.

**EROI-Negative** — English. When a system consumes more energy than it produces. The baseline for collapse. Extractive systems inevitably become EROI-negative as their resource base depletes. It matters because EROI-negative systems are drawing down the life-supporting capacity of the systems that sustain them. They are not defensible. They are extractive.

**EROI-Positive** — English. When a system produces more energy than it consumes. The baseline for a system that sustains rather than depletes living conditions. It matters because EROI-positive is the minimum threshold for regeneration. A system must give back more than it takes to be defensible.

**Extraction Apparatus** — English. The set of institutions, technologies, and incentive structures that enable and reward extraction. Includes the legal frameworks that treat land as commodity, the financial systems that demand growth, and the cultural norms that equate consumption with prosperity. It matters because defeating extraction requires dismantling its apparatus, not merely opposing individual extractive actors.

**Extractive** — English. A system that consumes more of its resource base than it replenishes. Extractive systems always fail eventually — they run out of what they are consuming. It matters because the choice between extractive and regenerative is the fundamental choice. Every governance decision either moves toward extraction or toward regeneration.

**Farmer Retention Rate** — English. The ratio of farmers entering a community versus exiting it per year, plus the rate at which succession (passing the farm to the next generation) is completed. It matters because a community with zero retention cannot build cumulative knowledge or improve practices across generations. Regeneration requires continuity. Continuity requires retention.

**Gift Economy** — English. An exchange system where goods and services are given without explicit agreement on return value or timing. Obligations are social, not contractual. It matters because gift economies can resist the equivalence trap that transforms giving into market exchange — but only when structurally designed to prevent accumulation and tallying.

**Haney Test** — English (named after its developer, Rick Haney). A commercial soil test that measures biological activity by analyzing soil respiration and water-extractable carbon and nitrogen. More comprehensive than standard soil tests that measure only mineral content. It matters because biological activity is what makes soil regenerative. Mineral-only tests miss the living part of soil.

**Haudenosaunee** — Iroquois language (Haudenosaunee Confederacy). The people of the Longhouse — the Iroquois Confederacy comprising the Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and Tuscarora nations. They developed the Great Law of Peace and a model of governance based on consensus, the role of women in selecting leaders, and the concept of considering the impact of decisions seven generations into the future. It matters because Haudenosaunee governance principles influenced early American democratic thought and offer proven models for consensus-based, long-term stewardship.

**Infiltration Rate** — English. The speed at which water enters soil, typically measured in inches or millimeters per hour. It matters because low infiltration means water runs off rather than soaking in — causing erosion, reducing water availability to plants, and preventing groundwater recharge. High infiltration indicates healthy, porous soil with good structure.

**Kibbutz** — Hebrew (kibbutz, collective settlement). A collective community in Israel that holds agricultural land in common. Decisions made by general assembly — one vote per adult member. Land cannot be sold or subdivided. It matters because it is the largest proven scale model of working agricultural land held in common. Over 270 kibbutzim managing roughly 20% of Israeli agricultural output demonstrates the model works across generations.

**Kula Ring** — Trobriand language (kula, exchange). A ceremonial exchange system among the Trobriand Islanders of Papua New Guinea involving shell necklaces and armbands that circulate in a fixed direction. The exchange creates social prestige through giving, not accumulation. It matters because it demonstrates that gift economies can operate at scale with ceremonial reinforcement preventing equivalence thinking and accumulation.

**Mana** — Polynesian languages (Mana). A spiritual force or power that resides in people, objects, and places. Can be inherited, earned through achievement, or lost through violation. It matters because mana provides a non-market mechanism for enforcing standards. Loss of mana — through violation of taboos or failure of stewardship — is a consequence that does not require an external enforcer.

**Meidung** — German (Amish dialect). The formal exclusion of an Amish member from the church and community for serious infractions. Excluded members cannot participate in community life, shared meals, or economic cooperation. It matters because it demonstrates that social exclusion as a consequence scale to modern tight-knit communities and produces genuine behavioral change without violence.

**Micronutrient Density** — English. The concentration of trace minerals and vitamins in food — iron, zinc, selenium, B-vitamins, and others. Distinct from macronutrient density (protein, fat, carbohydrates). It matters because EROI-positive food that lacks micronutrient density does not sustain health. A regenerative food system must produce food that nourishes, not merely calories.

**NEROI** — English acronym: Net Energy Return on Invested. The absolute amount of usable energy left after all energy costs are paid. While EROI is a ratio, NEROI is the actual quantity available. A system can have high EROI but low NEROI if the total scale is small. It is the anchor metric for measuring whether a system is regenerative.

**Net Energy** — English. The usable energy left after subtracting the cost of getting it. Formula: Net Energy = Energy Delivered minus Energy Cost to Deliver. This is what sustains living systems. It matters because if net energy declines, living systems are being depleted. Net energy is what remains after the cost of production is paid. It is the true product of any system.

**Pollinator Index** — English. A measure of pollinator biodiversity — the number and variety of bee and butterfly species present in an ecosystem. Measured by volunteer observation using tools like the Merlin app, comparing year-over-year counts. It matters because pollinator diversity signals improving soil biology. A farm losing pollinators is losing the biological foundation of its soil. Pollinator recovery indicates regeneration.

**Reciprocity** — English (from Latin reciprocus, returning). The practice of exchanging things with others for mutual benefit. In gift economies, reciprocity is social — you give and expect to receive, but the timing and value are not specified. It matters because reciprocity is the social glue of commons. Without some form of reciprocity, commons institutions cannot sustain member participation.

**Regenerative** — English. A system that produces more life-supporting capacity over time rather than consuming or degrading it. The goal is net positive, not net neutral. It matters because sustainable means keeping things the same. Regenerative means making things better. The ambition level matters. Systems that merely sustain are not regenerative — they are static.

**Regenerative Covenant** — English. A formal agreement among community members binding them to regenerative practices — no-till farming, pollinator habitat maintenance, water cycling standards, soil building protocols. It matters because covenants transform regenerative intent into enforceable obligations. A community covenant is the governance layer that makes the physical practices stick.

**Shame/Saturation** — English. In oral or tight communities, the consequence of public acknowledgment of violation — standing is destroyed, reputation is permanently marked. Saturation refers to the full-cycle effect: the community is saturated with knowledge of the violation, making it impossible for the violator to rebuild reputation without a formal restoration process. It matters because reputation is the primary currency in communities without market mechanisms. Shame is the enforcement mechanism that protects the commons without violence.

**Social Exclusion (Shunning)** — English. The practice of partially or fully excluding a violator from community participation. In tight communities, exclusion means exclusion from economic opportunity, shared resources, and mutual aid. It matters because it is the graduated consequence that precedes removal. Calibrated correctly, it is painful enough to change behavior without being destructive enough to create martyrs.

**Soil Health Score** — English. A composite metric of soil biological activity, typically measured by the Haney Test. Includes soil respiration (CO2 released by microbes), water-extractable carbon, and water-extractable nitrogen. It matters because healthy soil is the foundation of regenerative agriculture. Soil health improves before NEROI improves — it is a leading indicator.

**Soil Respiration** — English. The rate at which soil microbes produce carbon dioxide (CO2). Higher respiration indicates more active microbial life. Measured by the Haney Test. It matters because microbial activity is what makes soil alive. Respiration is the heartbeat of the soil ecosystem. Low respiration means dead soil.

**Species Richness** — English. The number of different species present in an ecosystem. Distinct from species abundance (how many individuals of each species). It matters because diversity provides resilience — each species fills a niche. A species-rich farm ecosystem is more resilient to pest outbreaks, climate variability, and disease than a monoculture. Species richness is a marker of ecosystem health.

**Stewardship** — English (from Old English stigweard, house guardian). The responsible management of resources for the benefit of current and future generations. Distinct from ownership. The steward does not own — they hold in trust and are accountable for the condition of what they hold. It matters because the stewardship framework separates use rights from destruction rights. A steward can use and must care. An owner can use or destroy.

**Tapu** — Hawaiian. A Polynesian concept meaning spiritual restriction or prohibition. Something tapu is set apart, sacred, not to be touched or approached. Violators become tapu — spiritually contaminated. Other members avoid contact to avoid catching the pollution. It matters because it is a self-enforcing consequence. No enforcer is required. The cosmologic framing creates automatic social distance.

**Tikanga** — Māori. Customary law and practice. The body of rules, protocols, and values that govern Māori social and cultural life. **whenua** — Māori. Land. Literally translates as "the placenta" — the afterbirth that connects a person to the whenua (land) from which they came. In Māori philosophy, whenua is an ancestor. People do not own land. Land owns people — in the sense that people are descended from and responsible to the land. It matters because whenua naming demonstrates that the land-as-kin framework is not metaphor — it is a fully developed philosophy with practical governance implications.
